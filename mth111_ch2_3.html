Solving Equations using the Multiplication & Division Property of Equality

Solving equations using the Multiplication & Division Property of Equality is very similar to the property we learned in the previous section. Now, instead of adding or subtracting, we divide or multiply.

Before we get there, let's recall that any number divided by itself equals one. For example:

\[4 \div 4 = \frac{4}{4} = 1\]

Let's also remember that the multiplicative inverse of a number — also called its reciprocal — multiplies with the original number to give one. For example:

\[\frac{1}{4} \cdot 4 = \frac{4}{4} = 1 \qquad \text{and} \qquad \frac{5}{3} \cdot \frac{3}{5} = \frac{15}{15} = 1\]

Steps to Solving Equations

  1. Start by drawing a line straight through the equal sign.
  2. Get the variable alone by moving the number to the other side.
    • To do this, perform the opposite operation on both sides. If the variable is multiplied by a number, divide both sides by that number; if it is divided, multiply both sides — or multiply both sides by the reciprocal.
  3. Check your solution.

Note: You can do whatever you want to an equation as long as you do it to both sides.

Example 1

Solve \(3x = 7\).

Solution

\(3x \mid = 7\) Draw a line through the equal sign to separate the two sides.
\(\dfrac{3x}{3} \mid = \dfrac{7}{3}\) Since \(x\) is multiplied by 3, divide both sides by 3.
\(x = \dfrac{7}{3}\) The variable is now isolated.
Check: \(3\!\left(\dfrac{7}{3}\right) = 7\)  \(\Rightarrow\)  \(7 = 7\) ✓ Substitute \(x = \dfrac{7}{3}\) back into the original equation. The statement is true.

The solution is \(x = \dfrac{7}{3}\).

Example 2

Solve \(6 = \dfrac{x}{5}\).

Solution

\(6 \mid = \dfrac{x}{5}\) Draw a line through the equal sign to separate the two sides.
\(5 \cdot 6 \mid = \dfrac{x}{5} \cdot 5\) Since \(x\) is divided by 5, multiply both sides by 5.
\(30 = x\) The variable is now isolated.
Check: \(6 = \dfrac{30}{5}\)  \(\Rightarrow\)  \(6 = 6\) ✓ Substitute \(x = 30\) back into the original equation. The statement is true.

The solution is \(x = 30\).

Example 3

Solve \(5 = \dfrac{2}{3}x\).

Solution

\(5 \mid = \dfrac{2}{3}x\) Draw a line through the equal sign to separate the two sides.
\(\dfrac{3}{2} \cdot 5 \mid = \dfrac{2}{3}x \cdot \dfrac{3}{2}\) Since \(x\) is multiplied by \(\dfrac{2}{3}\), multiply both sides by the reciprocal \(\dfrac{3}{2}\).
\(\dfrac{15}{2} = x\) The variable is now isolated.
Check: \(5 = \dfrac{2}{3} \cdot \dfrac{15}{2}\)  \(\Rightarrow\)  \(5 = \dfrac{30}{6} = 5\) ✓ Substitute \(x = \dfrac{15}{2}\) back into the original equation. The statement is true.

The solution is \(x = \dfrac{15}{2}\).

Self Check A

Solve \(3x = -15\).

Divide both sides by 3:

\[\frac{3x}{3} = \frac{-15}{3}\] \[x = -5\]

Self Check B

Solve \(\dfrac{x}{-4} = -3\).

Multiply both sides by \(-4\):

\[(-4) \cdot \frac{x}{-4} = (-4) \cdot (-3)\] \[x = 12\]

Self Check C

Solve \(\dfrac{4}{5}x = \dfrac{6}{7}\).

Multiply both sides by the reciprocal \(\dfrac{5}{4}\):

\[\frac{5}{4} \cdot \frac{4}{5}x = \frac{5}{4} \cdot \frac{6}{7}\] \[x = \frac{30}{28} = \frac{15}{14}\]

Summary

To solve an equation using the Multiplication and Division Property of Equality, isolate the variable by performing the opposite operation on both sides. If the variable is multiplied by a number, divide both sides by that number. If the variable is divided by a number, multiply both sides by that number. When the coefficient is a fraction, multiply both sides by its reciprocal. Always check your solution by substituting it back into the original equation.
