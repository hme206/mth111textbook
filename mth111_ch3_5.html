Chapter 3: Measurements & Conversions

Section 3.5: The Metric System

Learning Objective(s)

  1. Describe the general relationship between U.S. customary units and metric units of length, weight/mass, and volume.
  2. Define the metric prefixes and use them to perform basic conversions among metric units.

Introduction

In the United States, both the U.S. customary measurement system and the metric system are used, especially in medical, scientific, and technical fields. In most other countries, the metric system is the primary system of measurement. If you travel to other countries, you will see that road signs list distances in kilometers and milk is sold in liters. People in many countries use words like "kilometer," "liter," and "milligram" to measure the length, volume, and weight of different objects. These measurement units are all part of the metric system.

Unlike the U.S. customary system of measurement, the metric system is based on 10s. For example, a liter is 10 times larger than a deciliter, and a centigram is 10 times larger than a milligram. This idea of "10" is not present in the U.S. customary system — there are 12 inches in a foot, 3 feet in a yard, and 5,280 feet in a mile!

What is Metric?

The metric system uses units such as meter, liter, and gram to measure length, liquid volume, and mass, just as the U.S. customary system uses feet, quarts, and ounces. In addition to different base units, the metric system is based on powers of 10 — a meter is 100 times larger than a centimeter, and a kilogram is 1,000 times heavier than a gram.

In the metric system, the basic unit of length is the meter — slightly larger than a yardstick, or just over three feet. The basic metric unit of mass is the gram; a regular-sized paperclip has a mass of about 1 gram. Note that in science and technical fields, a distinction is made between weight and mass: weight measures the pull of gravity on an object and would differ on the moon, while mass measures the amount of substance and remains constant. For everyday purposes on Earth, mass and weight can be used fairly interchangeably. The basic metric unit of volume is the liter — slightly larger than a quart.

Prefixes in the Metric System

The metric system is a base-10 system, meaning each successive unit is 10 times larger than the previous one. The names of metric units are formed by adding a prefix to the basic unit of measurement. To tell how large or small a unit is, look at the prefix. To tell whether the unit measures length, mass, or volume, look at the base word.

Converting Units Up and Down the Metric Scale

Converting between metric units of measure requires knowledge of the metric prefixes and an understanding of the decimal system. In the prefix scale above, each unit is 10 times larger than the one immediately to its right. This means:

  • Moving down (toward smaller units) → multiply by 10 for each step.
  • Moving up (toward larger units) → divide by 10 for each step.

Example 1

How many milligrams are in one decigram?

Solution

Locate decigrams (dg) and milligrams (mg) on the prefix scale:

kg  –  hg  –  dag  –  g  –  dg  –  cg  –  mg

Decigrams are larger than milligrams, so we expect many milligrams in one decigram. Moving from dg to mg requires 2 steps to the right (toward smaller units), so we multiply by \(10 \times 10 = 100\):

\[1\text{ dg} \times 10 \times 10 = 100\text{ mg}\]

There are 100 milligrams in 1 decigram.

Example 2

Convert 1 centimeter to kilometers.

Solution

Locate kilometers (km) and centimeters (cm) on the prefix scale:

km  –  hm  –  dam  –  m  –  dm  –  cm  –  mm

Kilometers are larger than centimeters, so we expect our answer to be less than 1. Moving from cm to km requires 5 steps to the left (toward larger units), so we divide by \(10^5 = 100{,}000\):

\[1\text{ cm} \div 10 \div 10 \div 10 \div 10 \div 10 = \frac{1}{100{,}000} = 0.00001\text{ km}\]

1 centimeter = 0.00001 kilometers.

Shortcut: Moving the Decimal Point

Once you understand the metric prefix scale, converting is as simple as moving the decimal point:

  • Converting to a smaller unit (moving right on the scale) → move the decimal point right by the number of steps.
  • Converting to a larger unit (moving left on the scale) → move the decimal point left by the number of steps.

For example, to convert 1 dg to mg (2 steps right): \(1\text{ dg} \rightarrow 100.\text{ mg}\)

To convert 1 cm to km (5 steps left): \(1\text{ cm} \rightarrow 0.00001\text{ km}\)

Self Check B

How many milliliters are in 1 liter?

Moving from liters to milliliters is 3 steps to the right on the prefix scale, so multiply by \(10 \times 10 \times 10 = 1{,}000\):

\[1\text{ L} \times 1{,}000 = 1{,}000\text{ mL}\]

Self Check C

Convert 3,085 milligrams to grams.

One gram is 1,000 times larger than a milligram, so move from mg to g is 3 steps to the left. Move the decimal point 3 places to the left:

\[3{,}085\text{ mg} \rightarrow 3.085\text{ g}\]

Example 3

Convert 7,225 centimeters to meters.

Solution

Meters is larger than centimeters, so we expect our answer to be less than 7,225. Using the factor label method, write 7,225 cm as a fraction and multiply by the appropriate unit fraction:

\[\frac{7{,}225\text{ cm}}{1} \cdot \frac{1\text{ m}}{100\text{ cm}} = \frac{7{,}225}{100}\text{ m} = 72.25\text{ m}\]

7,225 centimeters = 72.25 meters.

Example 4

If you have a prescription for 5,000 mg of medicine, and upon getting it filled the dosage label reads 5 g of medicine, did the pharmacist make a mistake?

Solution

Convert 5,000 mg to grams using the factor label method:

\[\frac{5{,}000\text{ mg}}{1} \cdot \frac{1\text{ g}}{1{,}000\text{ mg}} = \frac{5{,}000}{1{,}000}\text{ g} = 5\text{ g}\]

Since 5,000 mg = 5 g, the pharmacist did not make a mistake.

Self Check D

Convert 32.5 kilometers to meters.

Using the factor label method:

\[\frac{32.5\text{ km}}{1} \cdot \frac{1{,}000\text{ m}}{1\text{ km}} = 32{,}500\text{ m}\]

Using Metric Conversions to Solve Problems

Learning how to solve real-world problems using metric conversions is as important as learning how to do the conversions themselves. Mathematicians, scientists, nurses, and even athletes are often confronted with situations where they are presented with metric measurements and must make informed decisions based on that data.

To solve these problems effectively: understand the context, perform the necessary conversions, and then check the reasonableness of your answer.

Example 5

Marcus bought a 2-meter board and cut off a piece 1 meter and 35 cm long. How much board is left?

Solution

To subtract, we need all measurements in the same unit. Convert everything to centimeters.

Convert 2 meters to centimeters:

\[\frac{2\text{ m}}{1} \cdot \frac{100\text{ cm}}{1\text{ m}} = 200\text{ cm}\]

Convert the cut piece (1 meter + 35 cm) to centimeters:

\[1\text{ m} = 100\text{ cm} \quad \Rightarrow \quad 100\text{ cm} + 35\text{ cm} = 135\text{ cm}\]

Subtract to find what remains:

\[200\text{ cm} - 135\text{ cm} = 65\text{ cm}\]

There is 65 cm of board left.

Example 6

A faucet drips 10 mL every minute. How much water will be wasted in a week?

Solution

Use the factor label method to convert time units and find the total milliliters wasted in one week:

\[\frac{10\text{ mL}}{1\text{ min}} \cdot \frac{60\text{ min}}{1\text{ hr}} \cdot \frac{24\text{ hr}}{1\text{ day}} \cdot \frac{7\text{ days}}{1\text{ week}} = \frac{10 \times 60 \times 24 \times 7}{1}\text{ mL/week}\] \[= 100{,}800\text{ mL per week}\]

Convert to liters (move 3 steps up the metric scale — divide by \(10^3\)):

\[\frac{100{,}800\text{ mL}}{1} \cdot \frac{1\text{ L}}{1{,}000\text{ mL}} = 100.8\text{ L}\]

The faucet wastes about 100.8 liters each week.

Self Check

A bread recipe calls for 600 g of flour. How many kilograms of flour would you need to make 5 loaves?

First find the total grams needed for 5 loaves:

\[600\text{ g} \times 5 = 3{,}000\text{ g}\]

Convert to kilograms (3 steps up the scale — divide by \(10^3\)):

\[\frac{3{,}000\text{ g}}{1{,}000} = 3\text{ kg}\]

You will need 3 kg of flour to make 5 loaves.

Summary

The metric system is based on joining one of a series of prefixes — kilo-, hecto-, deka-, deci-, centi-, and milli- — with a base unit of measurement such as meter, liter, or gram. Units in the metric system are all related by a power of 10, which means each successive unit is 10 times larger than the previous one. This makes converting one metric measurement to another straightforward, often as simple as moving a decimal point. Always consider the direction of the conversion: converting to a smaller unit moves the decimal right (larger number), while converting to a larger unit moves the decimal left (smaller number). The factor label method can also be applied to metric conversions using unit fractions and cancellation.
