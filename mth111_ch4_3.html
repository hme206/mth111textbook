Section 4.3: Graphing Using Intercepts

Learning Objective(s)

  1. Identify the intercepts on a graph.
  2. Find the intercepts from an equation of a line.
  3. Graph a line using the intercepts.
  4. Choose the most convenient method to graph a line.

Identify the Intercepts on a Graph

The intercepts of a graph are the points at which the graph crosses the \(x\)- and \(y\)-axes.

The x-intercept is the point at which the graph crosses the \(x\)-axis. At this point, the \(y\)-coordinate is zero, so the x-intercept has the form \((x, 0)\).

The y-intercept is the point at which the graph crosses the \(y\)-axis. At this point, the \(x\)-coordinate is zero, so the y-intercept has the form \((0, y)\).

Intercepts of a Line

  • The x-intercept is where the line crosses the \(x\)-axis. It has the form \((x, 0)\).
  • The y-intercept is where the line crosses the \(y\)-axis. It has the form \((0, y)\).

Find the Intercepts from an Equation of a Line

To determine the x-intercept, substitute \(y = 0\) into the equation and solve for \(x\). To determine the y-intercept, substitute \(x = 0\) into the equation and solve for \(y\).

Let's find the intercepts of the equation \(y = 3x - 1\).

Finding the x-intercept: substitute \(y = 0\) and solve for \(x\).

\(y = 3x - 1\) Start with the original equation.
\(0 = 3x - 1\) Substitute \(y = 0\).
\(1 = 3x\) Add \(1\) to both sides.
\(\dfrac{1}{3} = x\) Divide both sides by \(3\).
x-intercept: \(\left(\dfrac{1}{3}, 0\right)\) Write the intercept as an ordered pair.

Finding the y-intercept: substitute \(x = 0\) and solve for \(y\).

\(y = 3x - 1\) Start with the original equation.
\(y = 3(0) - 1\) Substitute \(x = 0\).
\(y = 0 - 1\) Multiply \(3 \cdot 0 = 0\).
\(y = -1\) Simplify.
y-intercept: \((0, -1)\) Write the intercept as an ordered pair.

Looking at the graph of \(y = 3x - 1\) below, we can confirm that the line crosses the \(x\)-axis at \(\left(\frac{1}{3}, 0\right)\) and the \(y\)-axis at \((0, -1)\).

How To: Find the Intercepts from an Equation of a Line

  • To find the x-intercept, substitute \(y = 0\) into the equation and solve for \(x\).
  • To find the y-intercept, substitute \(x = 0\) into the equation and solve for \(y\).

Example 1

Find the intercepts of the equation \(y = -\dfrac{1}{3}x - 4\). Then use a graphing utility to verify your answer.

Solution

Finding the x-intercept: substitute \(y = 0\) and solve for \(x\).

\[\begin{align*} y &= -\frac{1}{3}x - 4 \\ 0 &= -\frac{1}{3}x - 4 \\ 4 &= -\frac{1}{3}x \\ -\frac{3}{1} \cdot 4 &= x \\ -12 &= x \end{align*}\]

The x-intercept is \((-12, 0)\).

Finding the y-intercept: substitute \(x = 0\) and solve for \(y\).

\[\begin{align*} y &= -\frac{1}{3}x - 4 \\ y &= -\frac{1}{3}(0) - 4 \\ y &= 0 - 4 \\ y &= -4 \end{align*}\]

The y-intercept is \((0, -4)\).

We can verify these answers graphically below. The line crosses the \(x\)-axis at \((-12, 0)\) and the \(y\)-axis at \((0, -4)\).

Choose the Most Convenient Method to Graph a Line

We have now seen two methods for graphing a line: plotting points and using intercepts. How do you decide which method to use for a given equation? Here are some guidelines:

Choosing the Most Convenient Method

  • If the equation is already solved for \(y\) (that is, in the form \(y = mx + b\)), the point-plotting method is often easiest — simply choose a few \(x\)-values and calculate \(y\).
  • If the equation is in the form \(Ax + By = C\), the intercept method is often most convenient — set each variable to zero in turn to find the two intercepts, then draw the line through them.
  • If the equation has only one variable (e.g., \(x = a\) or \(y = b\)), recognize it immediately as a vertical or horizontal line.

Remember: you only need two points to draw a line, but finding a third point is a good habit to catch errors. If your three points do not line up, recheck your algebra.
