Section 2.2: Solving Equations using the Addition & Subtraction Property

Learning Objective(s)

  1. Solve equations using the Subtraction and Addition Property of Equality.
  2. Translate and solve applications.

Introduction

As a kid you may have played on a teeter totter with another child at the playground. You may have even tried to balance the teeter totter so that you were both up in the air at the same time. Or perhaps you've tried to balance a pencil on your finger. Both of those tasks are difficult, but you know that if you get just the right amount of weight on each side you can balance them both. In this section, we will solve equations by keeping quantities on both sides of an equal sign in perfect balance.

Solving Equations using the Addition and Subtraction Property of Equality

Solving an equation is like solving a puzzle. The purpose in solving an equation is to find the value or values of the variable that makes each side of the equation the same. Any value of the variable that makes the equation true is called a solution to the equation. It is the answer to the puzzle.

Steps to Solving Equations

  1. Start by drawing a line straight through the equal sign.
  2. Get the variable alone by moving the number to the other side.
    • To do this, perform the opposite operation on both sides. If the number is added, subtract it from both sides; if it is subtracted, add it to both sides.
  3. Check your solution.

Note: You can do whatever you want to an equation as long as you do it to both sides.

Example 1

Solve \(x + 3 = 5\).

Solution

\(x + 3 \mid = 5\) Draw a line through the equal sign to separate the two sides.
\(\underline{-3 \mid\; = -3}\) Since \(+3\) is on the left, subtract 3 from both sides.
\(x = 2\) The variable is now isolated.
Check: \(2 + 3 = 5\)   \(\Rightarrow\)   \(5 = 5\) ✓ Substitute \(x = 2\) back into the original equation. The statement is true.

The solution is \(x = 2\).

Example 2

Solve \(4 = -6 + x\).

Solution

\(4 \mid = -6 + x\) Draw a line through the equal sign to separate the two sides.
\(\underline{+6 \mid\; = +6}\) Since \(-6\) is on the right, add 6 to both sides.
\(10 = x\) The variable is now isolated.
Check: \(4 = -6 + 10\)   \(\Rightarrow\)   \(4 = 4\) ✓ Substitute \(x = 10\) back into the original equation. The statement is true.

The solution is \(x = 10\).

Self Check A

Solve \(x + 5 = -6\).

Subtract 5 from both sides:

\[x + 5 - 5 = -6 - 5\] \[x = -11\]

Self Check B

Solve \(x - 3 = -10\).

Add 3 to both sides:

\[x - 3 + 3 = -10 + 3\] \[x = -7\]

Applications

Most of the time the hardest part about word problems is understanding what they are trying to say. Remember that an equation has an equal sign between two algebraic expressions. So if we have a sentence that tells us two phrases are equal, we can translate it into an equation. We look for clue words that mean "equals." Some words that translate to the equal sign are:

  • Is equal to
  • Is the same as
  • Is
  • Gives
  • Was
  • Will be

It may be helpful to put a box around the "equals" word(s) in the sentence to help you focus separately on each phrase. Then translate each phrase into an expression, and write them on each side of the equal sign.

Example 3

Translate the sentence into an algebraic equation: The sum of 6 and 9 is 15.

Solution

The sum of 6 and 9 is 15 Locate the "equal" word: is.
The sum of 6 and 9 \(= 15\) Write the \(=\) sign in place of is.
\(6 + 9 = 15\) Locate the operation word (sum) and translate into symbols.

The algebraic equation is \(6 + 9 = 15\).

Summary

To solve an equation using the Addition and Subtraction Property of Equality, isolate the variable by performing the opposite operation on both sides of the equation. Always check your solution by substituting it back into the original equation. Word problems can be translated into equations by identifying key "equals" words and translating phrases on each side of that word into algebraic expressions.

Applications

In the following exercises, solve each application problem.

  1. Vince's car insurance has a \(\$500\) deductible. Find the amount the insurance company will pay, \(p\), for an \(\$1800\) claim by solving the equation \(\$500 + p = \$1800\).
  2. Marta's homeowner's insurance policy has a \(\$750\) deductible. The insurance company paid \(\$5800\) to repair damages caused by a storm. Find the total cost of the storm damage, \(d\), by solving the equation \(d - \$750 = \$5800\).
  3. Arthur bought a suit that was on sale for \(\$120\) off. He paid \(\$340\) for the suit. Find the original price, \(p\), of the suit by solving the equation \(p - \$120 = \$340\).
  4. Rita bought a sofa that was on sale for \(\$1299\). She paid a total of \(\$1409\), including sales tax. Find the amount of the sales tax, \(t\), by solving the equation \(\$1299 + t = \$1409\).
