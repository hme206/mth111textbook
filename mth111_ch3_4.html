Chapter 3: Measurements & Conversions

Section 3.4: U.S. Measurement – Capacity

Learning Objective(s)

  1. Define units of capacity and convert from one to another.
  2. Perform arithmetic calculations on units of capacity.
  3. Solve application problems involving units of capacity.

Introduction

Capacity is the amount of liquid (or other pourable substance) that an object can hold when it's full. When a liquid, such as milk, is being described in gallons or quarts, this is a measure of capacity.

Understanding units of capacity can help you solve problems like this: Sven and Johanna were hosting a potluck dinner. They did not ask their guests what they would be bringing, and three people ended up bringing soup. Erin brought 1 quart, Richard brought 3 pints, and LeVar brought 9 cups. How many cups of soup did they have all together?

Units of Capacity

There are five main units for measuring capacity in the U.S. customary measurement system. The smallest unit of measurement is a fluid ounce. Note that "ounce" is also used as a measure of weight, so it is important to use the word "fluid" when talking about capacity. Sometimes the prefix "fluid" is omitted when it is clear from context that the measurement refers to capacity rather than weight.

Units of Capacity — Descriptions

  • Fluid Ounce — A unit of capacity equal to \(\tfrac{1}{8}\) of a cup. One fluid ounce of water at 62°F weighs about one ounce. The amount of liquid medicine is often measured in fluid ounces.
  • Cup — A unit equal to 8 fluid ounces. The capacity of a standard measuring cup is one cup.
  • Pint — A unit equal to 16 fluid ounces, or 2 cups. The capacity of a carton of ice cream is often measured in pints.
  • Quart — A unit equal to 32 fluid ounces, or 4 cups. You often see quarts of milk being sold in the supermarket.
  • Gallon — A unit equal to 4 quarts, or 128 fluid ounces. When you fill up your car with gasoline, the price of gas is often listed in dollars per gallon.

You can use any of these five measurement units to describe the capacity of an object, but it makes more sense to use certain units for certain purposes. For example, it makes more sense to describe the capacity of a swimming pool in gallons and the capacity of an expensive perfume in fluid ounces.

Sometimes you will need to convert between units of measurement. For example, you might want to express 5 gallons of lemonade in cups if you are trying to determine how many 8-fluid-ounce servings the lemonade would yield. Converting Between Units of Capacity

As with converting units of length and weight, you can use the factor label method to convert from one unit of capacity to another. When the table does not contain a direct conversion factor between two units, use an intermediate unit and chain the conversion factors together.

Example 1

How many pints is \(3\tfrac{3}{4}\) gallons?

Solution

Since a gallon is larger than a pint, we expect our answer in pints to be greater than \(3\tfrac{3}{4}\). The table does not contain a direct conversion factor for gallons to pints, so we use quarts as an intermediate unit:

\[\frac{11}{4}\text{ gal} \cdot \frac{4\text{ qt}}{1\text{ gal}} \cdot \frac{2\text{ pt}}{1\text{ qt}}\]

The units "gallons" and "quarts" cancel, leaving pints:

\[= \frac{11 \times 4 \times 2}{4 \times 1 \times 1}\text{ pt} = \frac{88}{4}\text{ pt} = 22\text{ pt}\]

\(3\tfrac{3}{4}\) gallons is equal to 22 pints.

Example 2

How many gallons is 32 fluid ounces?

Solution

Since gallons is a larger unit than fluid ounces, we expect our answer to be less than 32. There is no direct conversion from fluid ounces to gallons, so we chain through cups, pints, and quarts:

\[\frac{32\text{ fl oz}}{1} \cdot \frac{1\text{ cup}}{8\text{ fl oz}} \cdot \frac{1\text{ pt}}{2\text{ cups}} \cdot \frac{1\text{ qt}}{2\text{ pt}} \cdot \frac{1\text{ gal}}{4\text{ qt}}\]

All intermediate units cancel, leaving gallons:

\[= \frac{32 \times 1 \times 1 \times 1 \times 1}{1 \times 8 \times 2 \times 2 \times 4}\text{ gal} = \frac{32}{128}\text{ gal} = \frac{1}{4}\text{ gal}\]

32 fluid ounces is the same as \(\tfrac{1}{4}\) gallon.

Self Check A

Find the sum of 4 gallons and 2 pints. Express your answer in cups.

Each gallon has 16 cups, so \(4 \times 16 = 64\) cups in 4 gallons. Each pint has 2 cups, so \(2 \times 2 = 4\) cups in 2 pints.

\[64 + 4 = 68\text{ cups}\]

Applying Unit Conversions

There are times when you will need to combine measurements that are given in different units. In order to do this, you need to convert first so that all quantities share the same unit. The problem itself may not specify which unit to use — in that case, choose the one that makes the computation most straightforward.

Example 3

Sven and Johanna were hosting a potluck dinner. Three people ended up bringing soup: Erin brought 1 quart, Richard brought 3 pints, and LeVar brought 9 cups. How much soup did they have in total?

Solution

To find the total, we need to add all three quantities. Before adding we must convert them all to the same unit. We'll convert everything to cups since LeVar's amount is already in cups.

Convert 1 quart to cups (1 quart = 4 cups):

\[1\text{ qt} \times \frac{4\text{ cups}}{1\text{ qt}} = 4\text{ cups}\]

Convert 3 pints to cups:

\[3\text{ pt} \times \frac{2\text{ cups}}{1\text{ pt}} = 6\text{ cups}\]

Now add all three quantities:

\[4\text{ cups} + 6\text{ cups} + 9\text{ cups} = 19\text{ cups}\]

There are 19 cups of soup for the dinner.

Example 4

Natasha is making lemonade to bring to the beach. She has two containers: one holds one gallon and the other holds 2 quarts. If she fills both containers, how many cups of lemonade will she have?

Solution

First, find the total capacity in quarts. Since 1 gallon = 4 quarts:

\[4\text{ qt} + 2\text{ qt} = 6\text{ qt}\]

Now convert 6 quarts to cups, chaining through pints:

\[\frac{6\text{ qt}}{1} \cdot \frac{2\text{ pt}}{1\text{ qt}} \cdot \frac{2\text{ cups}}{1\text{ pt}} = \frac{6 \times 2 \times 2}{1}\text{ cups} = 24\text{ cups}\]

Alternatively, you could convert directly: 1 gallon = 16 cups and 2 quarts = 8 cups, giving \(16 + 8 = 24\) cups.

Natasha will have 24 cups of lemonade.

Self Check B

Alan is making chili using a recipe that makes 24 cups of chili. He has a 5-quart pot and a 2-gallon pot, and is trying to determine whether the chili will all fit in one of these pots. Which pot will fit the chili?

Convert each pot's capacity to cups:

\[5\text{ qt} \times \frac{4\text{ cups}}{1\text{ qt}} = 20\text{ cups}\] \[2\text{ gal} \times \frac{16\text{ cups}}{1\text{ gal}} = 32\text{ cups}\]

The 5-quart pot holds only 20 cups, so 24 cups of chili will not fit. The 2-gallon pot holds 32 cups, so 24 cups of chili will fit in the 2-gallon pot.

Summary

There are five basic units for measuring capacity in the U.S. customary measurement system: the fluid ounce, cup, pint, quart, and gallon. These units are related to one another, and capacity can be described using any of them. Typically, people use gallons to describe larger quantities and fluid ounces, cups, pints, or quarts for smaller quantities. Often, in order to compare or solve problems involving the amount of liquid in a container, you need to convert from one unit of measurement to another using the factor label method.
