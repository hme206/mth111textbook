Chapter 2: The Language of Algebra

Section 2.8: Variation

Learning Objective(s)

  1. Solve applications involving Direct Variation.
  2. Solve applications involving Inverse Variation.

Direct Variation

In every relationship there is a dependent and an independent variable. When the independent variable has a direct influence on the dependent variable — meaning one quantity is a constant multiplied by another — each variable is said to vary directly with the other.

Consider this example: a used-car company offers Nicole a sales position with 16% commission. Her earnings depend on her sales. If she sells a vehicle for \(\$4{,}600\), she earns \(\$736\). The formula \(y = 0.16x\) tells us her earnings \(y\) come from multiplying 16% (as a decimal) by the sale price \(x\). As the sale price increases, the earnings increase as well.

\(x\) — Sale Price \(y = 0.16x\) Interpretation
\(\$4{,}600\) \(y = 0.16(\$4{,}600) = \$736\) Nicole earns \(\$736\) commission for selling a \(\$4{,}600\) vehicle.
\(\$9{,}200\) \(y = 0.16(\$9{,}200) = \$1{,}472\) Nicole earns \(\$1{,}472\) commission for selling a \(\$9{,}200\) vehicle.
\(\$18{,}400\) \(y = 0.16(\$18{,}400) = \$2{,}944\) Nicole earns \(\$2{,}944\) commission for selling a \(\$18{,}400\) vehicle.

Direct Variation

If \(x\) and \(y\) are related by an equation of the form:

\[y = kx\]

then the relationship is a direct variation and \(y\) varies directly with \(x\). The constant \(k\) is called the constant of variation.

Steps to solve a direct variation problem:

  1. Identify the input \(x\) and the output \(y\).
  2. Determine the constant of variation \(k\) by dividing \(y\) by \(x\).
  3. Use \(k\) to write an equation for the relationship.
  4. Substitute known values into the equation to find the unknown.

Note: Direct variation problems can also be solved by setting up a proportion \(\dfrac{y_1}{x_1} = \dfrac{y_2}{x_2}\) and cross-multiplying.

Example 1

The quantity \(y\) varies directly with \(x\). If \(y = 25\) when \(x = 2\), find \(y\) when \(x = 6\).

Solution

Method 1: Constant of Variation

Write the equation. \(y = kx\) Start with the direct variation equation.
Substitute. \(25 = k \cdot 2\) Plug in the known values.
Solve for \(k\). \(k = \dfrac{25}{2}\) Divide both sides by 2.
Find \(y\). \(y = \dfrac{25}{2}(6) = 25(3) = 75\) Substitute \(k\) and \(x = 6\) into the equation.
Answer \(y = 75\)

Method 2: Proportion

Set up proportion. \(\dfrac{25}{2} = \dfrac{y}{6}\) Use \(\dfrac{y_1}{x_1} = \dfrac{y_2}{x_2}\).
Cross multiply. \(2y = 25(6) = 150\)
Divide. \(y = 75\)
Answer \(y = 75\)

Example 2

The velocity \(v\) of a falling object varies directly with the time \(t\) of the fall. If after 2 seconds the velocity is 64 feet per second, what is the velocity after 5 seconds?

Solution

Method 1: Constant of Variation

Write the equation. \(v = k \cdot t\)
Substitute. \(64 = k \cdot 2\)
Solve for \(k\). \(k = \dfrac{64}{2} = 32\)
Find \(v\). \(v = 32(5) = 160 \text{ ft/sec}\) Substitute \(k = 32\) and \(t = 5\).
Answer \(v = 160\) ft/sec

Method 2: Proportion

Set up proportion. \(\dfrac{64}{2} = \dfrac{v}{5}\)
Cross multiply. \(2v = 64(5) = 320\)
Divide. \(v = 160\) ft/sec
Answer \(v = 160\) ft/sec Both methods give the same answer.

Self Check A

\(y\) varies directly as \(x\). When \(x = 3\), then \(y = 12\). Find \(y\) when \(x = 20\).

Using proportions: \(\dfrac{12}{3} = \dfrac{y}{20}\) → \(3y = 240\) → \(y = 40\).

\(y = 40\)

Self Check B

The distance \(s\) that an object falls varies directly with the time \(t\) of the fall. If an object falls 16 feet in one second, how long will it take to fall 144 feet?

Using proportions: \(\dfrac{16}{1} = \dfrac{144}{t}\) → \(16t = 144\) → \(t = 9\) seconds.

It will take 9 seconds to fall 144 feet.

Inverse Variation

With direct variation, as one variable increases the other also increases. However, sometimes as one variable increases the other decreases. When this happens we say that \(y\) varies inversely with \(x\) (also called indirect variation).

Water temperature in an ocean varies inversely with the water's depth. Between depths of 250 and 500 feet, the formula \(T = \dfrac{14{,}000}{d}\) gives the temperature in degrees Fahrenheit at depth \(d\) in feet. As depth increases, temperature decreases.

\(d\) — Depth \(T = \dfrac{14{,}000}{d}\) Interpretation
500 ft \(T = \dfrac{14{,}000}{500} = 28\) At a depth of 500 ft, the water temperature is 28°F.
350 ft \(T = \dfrac{14{,}000}{350} = 40\) At a depth of 350 ft, the water temperature is 40°F.
250 ft \(T = \dfrac{14{,}000}{250} = 56\) At a depth of 250 ft, the water temperature is 56°F.

Inverse Variation

If \(x\) and \(y\) are related by an equation of the form:

\[y = \frac{k}{x}\]

then the relationship is an inverse variation and \(y\) varies inversely with \(x\). Sometimes the word indirect is used in place of inverse.

Steps to solve an inverse variation problem:

  1. Identify the input \(x\) and the output \(y\).
  2. Determine the constant of variation \(k\) by multiplying \(y\) by \(x\).
  3. Use \(k\) to write an equation for the relationship.
  4. Substitute known values into the equation to find the unknown.

Note: Inverse variation problems can also be solved using the product rule \(x_1 y_1 = x_2 y_2\).

Example 3

A quantity \(y\) varies inversely with \(x\). If \(y = 25\) when \(x = 2\), find \(y\) when \(x = 6\).

Solution

Method 1: Constant of Variation

Write the equation. \(y = \dfrac{k}{x}\) Start with the inverse variation equation.
Substitute. \(25 = \dfrac{k}{2}\)
Solve for \(k\). \(k = 2(25) = 50\) Multiply both sides by 2.
Find \(y\). \(y = \dfrac{50}{6} \approx 8.33\) Substitute \(k = 50\) and \(x = 6\).
Answer \(y \approx 8.33\)

Method 2: Product Rule

Apply the rule. \(x_1 y_1 = x_2 y_2\)
Substitute. \(2(25) = 6y\)
Simplify. \(50 = 6y\)
Divide. \(y = \dfrac{50}{6} \approx 8.33\)
Answer \(y \approx 8.33\)

Example 4

The rate of vibration of a string \(v\) under constant tension varies inversely with the length of the string \(l\). If a string is 24 inches long and vibrates 128 times per second, what is the length of a string that vibrates 64 times per second?

Solution

Method 1: Constant of Variation

Write the equation. \(v = \dfrac{k}{l}\)
Substitute. \(128 = \dfrac{k}{24}\)
Solve for \(k\). \(k = 24(128) = 3{,}072\)
Find \(l\). \(64 = \dfrac{3{,}072}{l}\) Substitute \(k = 3{,}072\) and \(v = 64\).
Solve for \(l\). \(l = \dfrac{3{,}072}{64} = 48 \text{ inches}\)
Answer The string is 48 inches long.

Method 2: Product Rule

Apply the rule. \(x_1 y_1 = x_2 y_2\)
Substitute. \(24(128) = l \cdot 64\)
Simplify. \(3{,}072 = 64l\)
Divide. \(l = \dfrac{3{,}072}{64} = 48 \text{ inches}\)
Answer The string is 48 inches long.

Self Check C

A quantity \(y\) varies inversely with \(x\). If \(y = 12\) when \(x = 64\), find \(y\) when \(x = 36\).

Using the product rule: \(64(12) = 36y\) → \(768 = 36y\) → \(y \approx 21.33\).

\(y \approx 21.33\)

Self Check D

The volume of a gas held at constant temperature varies indirectly as the pressure of the gas. If the volume is 1,200 cubic cm when the pressure is 200 mm of mercury, what is the volume when the pressure is 300 mm of mercury?

Using the product rule: \(1{,}200(200) = 300v\) → \(240{,}000 = 300v\) → \(v = 800 \text{ cm}^3\).

The volume is \(800 \text{ cm}^3\).

Summary

Direct variation describes a relationship where \(y = kx\) — as one variable increases, the other increases proportionally. Inverse variation describes a relationship where \(y = \dfrac{k}{x}\) — as one variable increases, the other decreases. In both cases, \(k\) is the constant of variation. Direct variation problems can be solved using the constant of variation or by proportion; inverse variation problems can be solved using the constant of variation or the product rule \(x_1 y_1 = x_2 y_2\).
