Chapter 1: Numbers

Section 1.7: Understanding Exponents and Square Roots

Learning Objective(s)

  1. Evaluate expressions containing exponents.
  2. Write repeated factors using exponential notation.
  3. Find a square root of a perfect square.

Introduction

Exponents provide a special way of writing repeated multiplication. Numbers written using exponents have a specific form, with each part providing important information. Writing numbers using exponents can also save a lot of space. The inverse operation of multiplying a number by itself is called finding the square root of a number, which is helpful for problems involving the area of a square.

Understanding Exponential Notation

Exponential notation is a special way of writing repeated factors. It has two parts:

  • The base — the number being multiplied by itself.
  • The exponent (or power) — the small number written up and to the right of the base, telling how many times to use the base as a factor.

For example, \(7 \times 7\) can be written as \(7^2\), where 7 is the base and 2 is the exponent:

\[7^2 = 7 \times 7 = 49\]

Reading Exponential Notation

  • \(7^2\) is read "seven squared" — multiplying a number by itself is called squaring.
  • \(7^3\) is read "seven cubed" — raising a number to the power of 3.
  • \(2^5\) is read "two to the fifth power" or "two to the power of five."
  • \(8^4\) is read "eight to the fourth power."

To find the value of a number in exponential form, rewrite it as repeated multiplication and then multiply.

Example 1

Find the value of \(4^2\).

Solution

The base is 4 and the exponent is 2, so use 4 as a factor twice:

\[4^2 = 4 \times 4 = 16\]

Answer: \(4^2 = 16\)

Example 2

Find the value of \(2^5\).

Solution

The base is 2 and the exponent is 5, so use 2 as a factor five times:

\[ \begin{aligned} 2^5 &= 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 \\ &= 4 \times 2 \times 2 \times 2 \\ &= 8 \times 2 \times 2 \\ &= 16 \times 2 \\ &= 32 \end{aligned} \]

Answer: \(2^5 = 32\)

Self Check A

Find the value of \(4^3\).

\[4^3 = 4 \times 4 \times 4 = 64\]

Writing Repeated Multiplication Using Exponents

Writing repeated multiplication in exponential notation saves time and space. For example, \(5 \times 5 \times 5 \times 5\) can be written as \(5^4\) — 5 is the base (the number being multiplied) and 4 is the exponent (how many times 5 appears). Be sure to count the numbers, not the multiplication signs, when determining the exponent.

Example 3

Write \(7 \times 7 \times 7\) in exponential notation.

Solution

The base is 7 (the number being multiplied). The factor 7 appears 3 times, so the exponent is 3.

\[7 \times 7 \times 7 = 7^3\]

Answer: \(7^3\) — read "seven cubed."

Self Check B

Write \(10 \times 10 \times 10 \times 10 \times 10 \times 10\) in exponential notation.

The base is 10 and it appears 6 times, so the exponent is 6.

\[10 \times 10 \times 10 \times 10 \times 10 \times 10 = 10^6\]

Understanding and Computing Square Roots

Raising a number to the power of 2 is called squaring the number. The result of squaring a whole number is called a perfect square. A perfect square is any number that can be written as a whole number raised to the power of 2. For example, 9 is a perfect square because \(3^2 = 9\).

The inverse operation of squaring a number is called finding the square root. Finding a square root answers the question: "What number multiplied by itself gives me this number?" The square root of 25 is 5, because \(5 \times 5 = 25\). Square roots are written using the radical sign: \(\sqrt{\phantom{0}}\)

\[\sqrt{25} = 5\]

Example 4

Find \(\sqrt{81}\).

Solution

Ask: what number multiplied by itself equals 81?

\[9 \times 9 = 81\]

Answer: \(\sqrt{81} = 9\)

Self Check C

Find \(\sqrt{36}\).

Since \(6 \times 6 = 36\):

\[\sqrt{36} = 6\]

Summary

Exponential notation is a shorthand way of writing repeated multiplication of the same number. A number written in exponential notation has a base (the number being multiplied) and an exponent (how many times to use the base). Raising a base to the power of 2 is called squaring; raising to the power of 3 is called cubing. The inverse of squaring a number is finding its square root — ask yourself "what number can I multiply by itself to get this result?"
